# Crypto Sensei L2 Evaluation Guidelines

This document is the source of truth for creating **L2 project evaluations** for Crypto Sensei / Sensei Project Evaluations.

L2 evaluations are deeper due-diligence reports used after a project deserves more than an L1 screening. They should be more analytical than L1 reviews, but still concise enough to be usable by the team.

This file is a living document. Update it whenever the L2 evaluation standard changes.

---

## 1. Purpose

An L2 evaluation should answer:

> Is this project strong enough, safe enough, and strategically aligned enough to justify client partnership onboarding?

L2 is not just a longer L1. It must include scored analysis across core categories and a sharper view of commercial upside, user risk, reputational exposure, and campaign fit.

The report should balance:

- VC/backer credibility
- funding and investor quality
- partner and KOL associations
- team consistency and leadership strength
- business model and tokenomics, if relevant
- ecosystem and product economy
- technical credibility
- social/community health
- roadmap and execution risk
- partnership suitability
- final recommendation

---

## 2. Length and Depth

L2 reviews should be deeper than L1, but still practical.

Rules:

- Keep the exported Google Doc / Word version within **4 pages maximum**.
- Use the full L2 structure and scoring system.
- Avoid bloated paragraphs.
- Each major section should usually have **3–5 bullets max** before the score/conclusion.
- Keep each conclusion short: 2–4 sentences.
- Do not over-explain obvious facts.
- Do not repeat the same risk across every section unless it materially affects that category.

## 4. Scoring Rules

Each section is scored out of 5.

### 4.5–5.0

Excellent. Strong trust signals, strong execution, strong alignment, and low/manageable risk.

### 4.0–4.4

Strong. Clear positives with some diligence items or execution risks.

### 3.5–3.9

Good but not clean. Worth exploring, but requires caution or clear campaign controls.

### 3.0–3.4

Average / mixed. Some positives, but notable weaknesses or uncertainty.

### 2.0–2.9

Weak. Material concerns, unclear execution, poor transparency, or questionable sustainability.

### 1.0–1.9

Very weak. Serious red flags, poor fit, or high user/reputational risk.

### 0–0.9

Not viable. Major deception, broken model, or extreme risk.

---

## 5. Decision Labels

Use one final decision label.

### APPROVED

Use when the project is credible, useful, and partnership-worthy, with manageable risks.

Example:

> Decision: APPROVED for next steps in client partnership onboarding.

### SOFT APPROVED

Use when the project has real potential but needs strict scope, caution, or further clarification before a full campaign.

Example:

> Decision: SOFT APPROVED for next steps, provided the campaign remains education-first and risk disclosures are clear.

### DECLINED

Use when the project is not a strong fit, lacks sufficient transparency, has poor audience alignment, or carries too much reputational risk.

Example:

> Decision: DECLINED for client partnership onboarding.

### HARD DECLINE

Use when the project shows major user-harm risk, misleading claims, suspicious operations, impossible returns, fake team signals, or unacceptable reputation risk.

Example:

> Decision: HARD DECLINE. This should not move forward under any partnership structure.

## 6. Team Section Format Rule for L2

L2 team sections must follow the reference-evaluation style, not the L1 team-card style.

Use this structure:

```markdown
## Team, Leadership & Vision Evaluation

● Team Consistency: Confirmed alignment between official website documentation, corporate filings, and professional LinkedIn profiles.
● Company Overview: Founded in [year] and headquartered in [location], with [team size / offices / operating model].
● CEO [Name]:
   ○ [1 achievement or strength]
   ○ [1 achievement or strength]
   ○ LinkedIn: [link OR LinkedIn not found]
● CTO [Name]:
   ○ [1 achievement or strength]
   ○ [1 achievement or strength]
   ○ LinkedIn: [link OR LinkedIn not found]
● COO / CMO / Compliance Lead / Head of Markets [Name]:
   ○ [1 achievement or strength]
   ○ [1 achievement or strength]
   ○ LinkedIn: [link OR LinkedIn not found]
● Vision: [Short vision]
● Problem/Solution: [Short problem/solution]

Team, Leadership & Vision Score: X/5

Conclusion: [Short judgement]
```

Rules:

- Include LinkedIn links for all key leaders where found.
- If no LinkedIn is found, write exactly: `LinkedIn not found`.
- Each team member should have **1–2 bullets** on achievements, background, strengths, or strategic relevance.
- Do not include every employee. Prioritize founders, CEO, CTO/technical lead, compliance/legal lead, markets/product lead, COO/operations lead, and any unusually important strategic figure.
- Keep the section compact enough to fit the 4-page max export rule.
- Do not add unnecessary verification text after the LinkedIn line.

---

## 7. Team Link Verification Rule

Always include team profile links where available, preferably LinkedIn.

Before adding a team profile link:

- verify the person appears on the official project/team/company source or reliable project-linked source
- verify the LinkedIn profile belongs to the correct person
- verify the profile ties to the project directly, or that official project context confirms the same person/role
- never add guessed or pattern-generated LinkedIn URLs

If a LinkedIn cannot be found after checking, use:

> LinkedIn not found

Do not use lazy placeholders like “not verified” unless the user specifically asks for verification status. For eval output, use the clean format only.

---

## 8. Source and Link Style

The body of the evaluation should stay clean and copy-friendly.

Rules:

- Do not add clickable external reference links throughout the eval body.
- Mention source names plainly where useful: official website, CoinDesk, LinkedIn, Crunchbase, regulator registry, etc.
- Team LinkedIn links are the only external links that should appear inside the evaluation body.
- Do not include raw URL dumps in Company, Tech, Social, or Evaluation sections.
- If system-required citations are needed in chat output, keep them outside or as light as possible while staying compliant.

## 9. Economy / Tokenomics Handling

Use the section title:

> Economy / Tokenomics / Ecosystem Evaluation

Adapt the section based on project type.

### If the project has a token

Cover:

- token utility
- FDV and valuation reasonableness
- circulating supply
- unlock/vesting risk
- emissions and incentives
- staking or points system
- demand sinks
- liquidity and exchange listings
- sustainability of rewards

### If the project has no token

Cover:

- business model
- revenue model
- fee model
- no-token logic
- user acquisition strategy
- liquidity/counterparty economics if relevant
- sustainability without token incentives

### For yield / lending / carry products

Cover:

- source of yield
- variable vs fixed returns
- collateral/liquidation risk
- counterparty exposure
- lockups
- redemption risk
- whether campaign language could mislead users

Never frame yield as guaranteed unless it is legally and mechanically confirmed, and even then avoid promotional certainty.

---

## 10. High-Risk Category Rules

Apply extra diligence to:

- exchanges
- derivatives platforms
- tokenized securities
- lending / credit lines
- yield / carry products
- prop trading
- crypto cards and payment products
- cloud mining or mining-income products
- AI trading agents or automation tools
- anonymous teams handling user funds

For these categories, explicitly address:

- user fund custody
- jurisdiction restrictions
- liquidation risk
- withdrawal risk
- KYC/AML policy
- regulatory status
- issuer/counterparty risk
- social complaints if present
- whether the campaign should be education-first

---

## 11. Final Evaluation Style

The final evaluation should be direct and decision-driven.

Good style:

> Roxom is a strong L2 candidate because it has credible backers, visible leadership, serious infrastructure partners, and a differentiated Bitcoin-native capital markets thesis. The strongest strategic angle is Bitcoin as the denominator for global finance. The main concern is complexity: tokenized assets, futures, credit lines, and carry products require strict education and careful campaign framing.

Bad style:

> Roxom is revolutionizing global finance and redefining the future of Bitcoin through an unparalleled ecosystem.

Keep the final evaluation to:

- 2–4 concise paragraphs
- up to 4 strategic alignment requirements
- final decision line

---

## 12. Standing Rules Summary

For every L2 eval:

1. Use the full scored L2 structure.
2. Keep the exported Google Doc / Word version within 4 pages maximum.
3. Score each main section out of 5.
4. Include a total score out of 30.
5. Use clear decision labels: APPROVED, SOFT APPROVED, DECLINED, HARD DECLINE.
6. Team section must follow the L2 bullet format from the reference files.
7. Team members should have 1–2 achievement/strength bullets plus LinkedIn link.
8. If no LinkedIn is found, write exactly: `LinkedIn not found`.
9. Include team profile links where available; never guess links.
10. Do not add external reference links in the body except team LinkedIn links.
11. Mention source names plainly instead of linking them in the eval body.
12. Do not mention XRP unless the project is XRP/XRPL-related or the user asks for XRP alignment.
13. Be stricter with high-risk financial products.
14. Never casually promote leverage, yield, lending, or tokenized securities.
15. Final recommendation must protect Sensei’s reputation first.
