# Crypto Sensei L1 Evaluation Guidelines

This document is the source of truth for creating L1 project evaluations for Crypto Sensei / Sensei Project Evaluations. It is a living document and must be updated whenever the evaluation standard changes.

## 1. Purpose

An L1 evaluation is an initial screening report used to decide whether a Web3, crypto, fintech, AI, trading, creator-tool, or adjacent project should move forward for partnership onboarding.

The evaluation must answer one question clearly:

> Is this project worth moving forward, or should it be declined?

The report should balance legitimacy, team visibility, product usefulness, audience fit, regulatory risk, reputation risk, campaign potential, and the final recommendation.

## 2. Length and Depth

L1 reviews must be concise but complete.

Rules:

- Keep the exported Google Doc / Word version within 4 pages maximum.
- Do not use a scoring system for L1 reviews.
- Do not turn an L1 into an L2 deep dive unless explicitly requested.
- Include enough detail to support the decision without over-explaining.
- Pros, Cons, Synergies, Negative Synergies, and Content / Partnership Offerings must not exceed 4 bullet points each.
- Keep the Evaluation section short and decision-driven.

## 4. Writing Style

Write in the established Sensei evaluation style:

- direct
- analytical
- practical
- slightly blunt where needed
- partnership-focused
- risk-aware
- not hype-driven
- not academic
- not overly polished corporate fluff

The report should sound like an experienced crypto analyst giving an internal recommendation.

Preferred style:

> Roxom is credible, but the product stack is too advanced to promote lazily.

Avoid:

> Roxom is revolutionizing the future of finance through unmatched innovation.

## 5. External Reference Link Rule

Do not add clickable external reference links throughout the body of the evaluation.

Allowed:

- Mention source names or domains plainly.
- Team profile links are allowed and should be included where verified.
- Required system citations may still appear in chat output when necessary.

Avoid:

- Hyperlinked research citations throughout the body.
- Long URL dumps in company, product, social, or evaluation sections.
- Reference links attached to every factual claim.

## 6. Team Profile Link and Format Rule

Always include team profile links where available, preferably LinkedIn.

Critical rules:

- Never add guessed, pattern-generated, or unverified LinkedIn URLs.
- Do not treat a quick failed search as proof that no profile exists.
- If a LinkedIn profile is found and tied to the project/person, include it.
- If no LinkedIn profile is found after checking official team context and direct/profile-level search, write exactly: `LinkedIn not found`.
- Do not write anything after the LinkedIn line in the Team section.
- Do not add “Verified:” lines, source notes, or explanations after the LinkedIn line.

Before adding a team profile link:

- verify the person appears on the official project/team/company source or in a reliable project-linked source
- verify the profile opens or appears in reliable search results
- verify the profile belongs to the correct person
- verify the profile ties to the project directly through the profile role, official company page, official team page, or project-linked post

Required team format:

```markdown
➢ Borja Martel Seward — Co-Founder & CEO  
Official Roxom team page confirms Borja Martel Seward as Co-Founder & CEO. Public-facing founder and one of the strongest trust anchors for the project.  
LinkedIn: https://ar.linkedin.com/in/borja-martel
```

For team members with no found LinkedIn:

```markdown
➢ Tomas Milberg — VP of Operations  
Official Roxom team page confirms Tomas Milberg as VP of Operations.  
LinkedIn not found
```

## 7. Source Handling and Verification Rules

For all L1 evaluations, verify the project using current public information before writing.

Minimum checks:

- official website
- official link hub if provided
- X/Twitter
- LinkedIn/company page
- team profiles where possible
- legal entity or company registration where relevant
- regulator/register pages where relevant
- funding/backer announcements where relevant
- reputable third-party coverage where relevant
- social/review sentiment where relevant

For higher-risk sectors, verify more deeply:

- exchanges
- derivatives platforms
- yield platforms
- prop trading
- crypto cards/payments
- tokenized securities
- DeFi protocols
- anything asking users to deposit funds

If something cannot be verified, say so plainly.

Use phrases such as:

- publicly presented as
- appears to
- could not verify
- needs clarification during onboarding
- not automatically negative, but should be checked

Do not overstate certainty.

## 8. XRP Mention Rule

Do not mention XRP unless:

- the project explicitly supports XRP
- the project is building on XRP/XRPL
- XRP is central to the partnership offer
- the user specifically asks to assess XRP alignment

Do not force XRP alignment into unrelated project evaluations.

For non-XRP projects, use broader audience-fit language instead:

- macro-focused audience
- serious crypto investors
- long-term holders
- Sensei’s mature investor demographic
- Bitcoin-focused audience
- trading-heavy segment

## 9. Decision Labels

Use one final decision label exactly as written.

### Soft approve

Use when the project has real value and can move forward, but only with additional verification, tighter campaign scope, explicit conditions, or stronger risk controls.

Final line example:

> Decision: Soft approve for next steps, provided the campaign remains education-first and the outstanding diligence items are resolved.

### Strongly approved

Use when the project is credible, strategically aligned, useful to the audience, and partnership-worthy with only manageable risks.

Final line example:

> Decision: Strongly approved for next steps in client partnership onboarding.

### Soft Decline

Use when the project should not proceed in its current form because of fit, transparency, execution, or risk concerns, but could be reconsidered if specific weaknesses are resolved.

Final line example:

> Decision: Soft Decline pending stronger transparency, clearer regulatory positioning, and resolution of the identified risks.

### Declined

Use when there are major legitimacy, user-safety, legal, reputational, deception, or severe viability concerns, or when the partnership is clearly unsuitable.

Final line example:

> Decision: Declined for client partnership onboarding.

## 10. Evaluation Logic

Always assess these layers.

### Legitimacy

- Is the team visible?
- Is the company real?
- Are claims verifiable?
- Are registrations/licenses real and relevant?
- Are backers real?
- Are there identity or company-structure concerns?

### Product Utility

- Does the product solve a real problem?
- Is it differentiated?
- Is it production-ready or mostly narrative?
- Is there user value beyond speculation?

### Business Model and Risk

- How does the project make money?
- Does the yield/return model make sense?
- Is it relying on deposits, emissions, referrals, or new-user inflows?
- Are there hidden withdrawal, liquidation, KYC, or lockup risks?

### Audience Fit

- Does this fit Sensei’s audience?
- Is it too technical, too risky, too off-category, or too geographically restricted?
- Is the product useful to mature investors, active traders, builders, creators, or another relevant audience segment?

### Reputational Risk

- What happens if users lose money?
- What happens if withdrawals fail?
- What happens if support is poor?
- What happens if regulators object?
- Would Sensei be blamed for user harm?

### Campaign Potential

- What content can be made safely?
- Is the best campaign educational, affiliate-led, media-led, giveaway-led, or onboarding-led?
- What disclaimers are required?

## 11. Red Flag Patterns

Strong decline indicators include:

- anonymous team handling user funds
- cloned or mismatched company registration
- unverifiable executive identities
- unrealistic fixed daily returns
- high APY without transparent mechanism
- withdrawal complaints or account freezes
- suspicious review manipulation
- no regulator registration where required
- broken or fake team links
- pressure for fast promotion
- unclear custody model
- misleading no-risk language
- guaranteed passive income claims
- heavy multi-level referral structure
- users must use VPNs to access restricted services

Use **Soft Decline** when the concerns may be remediable. Use **Declined** when the concerns are severe, deceptive, unsafe, or fundamentally unsuitable.

## 12. Green Flag Patterns

Positive indicators include:

- visible founders with relevant track records
- verified LinkedIn/team profiles
- credible funding/backers
- real product with active users
- clear legal entity
- real regulatory registration where relevant
- no unrealistic yield claims
- transparent custody/security model
- reputable security audits
- strong user sentiment from credible sources
- professional social presence
- clear campaign fit
- useful educational angle
- no pressure for irresponsible promotion

## 13. High-Risk Category Rules

### Exchanges / Derivatives Platforms

Must check:

- jurisdiction restrictions
- leverage levels
- KYC policy
- withdrawal complaints
- regulatory registration
- proof of reserves / security claims
- whether US/UK/EU users are restricted

Never promote as easy profit.

### Yield / Lending / Carry Products

Must check:

- source of yield
- sustainability
- collateral/liquidation risk
- variable vs guaranteed rates
- lockups
- counterparty exposure
- smart contract exposure

Never use guaranteed-yield language.

### Tokenized Securities

Must check:

- issuer
- custody model
- jurisdiction
- redemption rights
- whether users own actual securities or tokenized exposure
- regional restrictions

Always explain that tokenized securities are not the same as ordinary brokerage ownership unless verified otherwise.

### AI / SaaS Tools

Must check:

- billing complaints
- refund policy
- support quality
- whether the product is real or mostly wrapper hype
- social proof quality
- utility for Sensei or the audience

### Hardware Wallets / Security Tools

Must check:

- custody model
- open-source vs closed-source
- secure element claims
- firmware update process
- supported assets
- user complaints
- supply-chain risk

## 14. Writing Standards by Section

### General

Keep factual. Include launch year, sector, description, and mission.

### Team

Use the required Team format exactly. Include the profile link when found. If no profile is found, write `LinkedIn not found`. Do not add anything after the LinkedIn line.

### Company

Clarify legal entity, jurisdiction, registrations, funding, and any corporate mismatch. If there is a discrepancy, flag it calmly.

> This is not automatically negative, but it should be clarified during onboarding because jurisdictional structure matters for a financial platform.

### Unique Qualities

Focus on what makes the project different, not generic marketing claims. Maximum 4 bullets.

### Partnership Assessment

Use pros and cons that matter to partnership risk and campaign fit. Maximum 4 pros and 4 cons.

### Synergies / Negative Synergies

Make these specific to Sensei’s audience and brand. Maximum 4 bullets each.

### Content / Partnership Offerings

Suggest realistic content routes. For risky products, prioritize education-first campaigns. Maximum 4 bullets.

### Status of Socials

Assess awareness, engagement, sentiment, and authenticity. Keep it short.

### Evaluation

This is the most important section. Keep it short, final, and decision-driven.

## 15. Default Campaign Framing by Risk Level

### Low-Risk Product

Examples: non-custodial tools, educational SaaS, research tools, hardware wallets.

Campaign can include tutorials, demos, affiliate links, discounts, and giveaways.

### Medium-Risk Product

Examples: trading tools, analytics, wallets with advanced features, tokenized non-custodial tools.

Campaign should include education, clear limitations, onboarding guidance, and risk disclaimers.

### High-Risk Product

Examples: exchanges, derivatives, yield, lending, tokenized securities, prop trading.

Campaign should start with education-first content, risk walkthroughs, eligibility disclosures, no aggressive deposit push, and no easy-money framing.

### Extreme-Risk Product

Examples: impossible-yield products, clone firms, unverifiable teams, or products with major user-harm indicators.

Campaign recommendation: no partnership. Consider only as an awareness case study if useful.

## 17. Current Standing Rules Summary

For every L1 evaluation:

1. Keep it concise enough for a maximum 4-page export.
2. Do not use a scoring system.
3. Do not mention XRP unless directly relevant.
4. Do not add external reference hyperlinks in the body.
5. Mention sources plainly when needed.
6. Always include team profile links where available.
7. Verify team links before adding them.
8. Do not include guessed LinkedIn links.
9. If no LinkedIn is found, write exactly: `LinkedIn not found`.
10. Do not add anything after the LinkedIn line in the Team section.
11. Pros, Cons, Synergies, Negative Synergies, and Content / Partnership Offerings must not exceed 4 bullets each.
12. Keep the established L1 structure.
13. Use only these decision labels: Soft approve, Strongly approved, Soft Decline, Declined.
14. Prioritize partnership suitability, audience safety, and reputational risk.
15. Be blunt about red flags.
16. For high-risk products, recommend education-first framing or decline.
17. The final decision must be obvious.

## 18. Final Principle

L1 evaluations should protect Sensei’s reputation first.

A project can be interesting and still be declined. A project can be useful and still require strict campaign controls. A project can have hype and still be a bad partnership. A project can lack hype and still be a strong fit.

The final recommendation should always be based on user safety, brand integrity, verifiable legitimacy, and campaign fit.
